Trolling variables: what actually moves your depth

Running depth is a physics problem with a lot of inputs. Our approach is to measure on the water while controlling as many of these variables as we can, then use physics and the latest AI models to cover the combinations no one could ever test by hand. Our measurements aren't perfect either. There will always be slight variation, and our numbers will keep improving as we test. What we can give you today is the most accurate representation we know how to build.

Speed

The biggest lever you control. Speed changes the balance between a lure's dive force and the drag on your line, so the same rig runs at different depths at 1.8 and 2.5 mph.

Line diameter

Thicker line drags more water and keeps a diving lure shallower on the same length of line. Moving from standard mono to thin braid can add several feet with no other change.

Line counter accuracy

Counters measure spool revolutions, not feet. Two reels with the same counter reading can have different amounts of line in the water if their spools are filled differently.

Spool fill

A full spool pays out more line per revolution than a half spool. Fill your reels the same way every time and your counter numbers become repeatable.

Mono stretch

Mono stretches under the pull of a working lure, so the line between you and the lure is longer and shaped differently than the counter suggests. Braid barely stretches at all.

Transducer depth

Your graph measures from the transducer, which sits below the waterline. Fish marked at 20 feet are deeper than 20 feet from the surface, where your lure numbers start.

Underwater current

GPS speed is boat speed over ground. Water moving beneath you means your lure's speed through the water is different, so the same pass can run deeper one direction than the other.

Water density

Yes, it's real. Cold water is denser and noticeably thicker than warm water, which changes the drag on your line and lure. A spring program and an August program aren't identical.

Lure tuning

Two identical crankbaits off the shelf can run differently. A lure that isn't tracking true wanders shallow. Tune it to run straight and it runs to its number.

Rod position

A rod tip held high pulls line up and out of the water; a tip near the surface puts more of it to work. Consistent rod placement keeps your numbers consistent.

The thought is simple: control your controllables first, and you'll be much more accurate. Fill your spools the same, rig your rods the same, hold your speed steady, and the variables you can't control get a lot smaller. Two rods rigged identically should run identically. That kind of repeatability is what puts lures in the zone and keeps them there.

Trolling depth questions, answered

How deep does my crankbait run?

It depends on the lure's bill and body, your line diameter, your trolling speed, and how much line you have out. The same crankbait can run several feet apart on different setups. Depth Vector models your exact combination and gives you a line out number for the depth you want.

Can AI really predict dive curves?

Not on its own, and we don't ask it to. Depth Vector starts with physics: how a lure's bill, your line, and your speed interact in the water. We then use the latest AI models to refine those curves against depths we measured ourselves, and to extend them across hundreds of thousands of combinations no one could ever test by hand. Measurement keeps it grounded; AI fills the gaps.

Why can't I just make bottom contact and go from there?

You can, and it works. Ticking bottom is a proven way to know exactly where your lure is. It only answers one question though: where the bottom is. When fish are suspended at 22 feet over 40, bottom contact can't take you to them, and dragging rock piles gets expensive. Depth Vector gives you the same confidence anywhere in the water column.

People have trolled without an app since the beginning of time. Why now?

They have, and plenty of fish hit the net that way. The app doesn't replace experience; it shortens the search. Instead of spending the first hour of a trip finding the zone, you start in it, and you can put a second and third rod on the same depth with confidence. Anglers happily spend hundreds on tackle for a chance at better odds. Knowing your depth costs about as much as two crankbaits a year.

How much leadcore line do I let out for 30 feet?

The old rule of thumb is that each color of leadcore adds roughly five feet of depth, so 30 feet would be about six colors. Real depth shifts with your speed, lure, and leader, so the rule is only a starting point. Depth Vector calculates colors out for your actual setup instead of an average.

Does trolling speed change lure depth?

Yes, and by more than most anglers expect. Speed changes the balance between a lure's dive force and the drag on your line, so the same rig runs at different depths at 1.8 and 2.5 mph. Weighted lines like leadcore ride noticeably higher as speed climbs. Depth Vector takes speed as an input, so your number updates when your speed does.

How accurate is Depth Vector?

As close as controlled measurement and physics can get, and we're upfront that nobody, including us, can promise perfection. Our numbers come from testing on the water while controlling every variable we can, and modeling the rest. Conditions will always add some variation. Keep your setup consistent and the numbers repeat, and repeatable is what puts fish in the boat.

What's the best app for trolling depth?

A good trolling depth app should be actively maintained, cover the ways you actually troll, and stand on real testing. Depth Vector was built for exactly that: advanced physics models refined with the latest AI against measured depths on the water, covering everything from a single crankbait to a full spread. We'd rather prove it on your boat than claim it here.
